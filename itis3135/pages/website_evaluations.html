Website Evaluations

Good Example

Foundry

Foundry.co is an e-commerce platform specializing in fine art prints, maps, blueprints, and typographic art, available both framed and unframed. Its homepage highlights new inventory, features categories such as Cartography, Architecture, and Astronomy, and showcases “Popular Now” items. Large imagery of prints reinforces the browsing experience, complemented by a clear “Shop All” call to action.

  • Contrast

    The site uses strong visual contrast: large images of prints against white background, bold typography for headings (“Shop All,” category names), clear call-to-action buttons. The art images provide visual interest and help draw the eye.

  • Repetition

    The site maintains consistency in style: repeated use of imagery blocks, category labels, consistent heading styles, button styling, grid layouts of art cards, etc. This gives a cohesive feel.

  • Alignment

    Elements are well aligned in a grid arrangement: product cards, headers, nav items have consistent alignment. Margins and paddings are fairly consistent.

  • Proximity

    Related items are grouped: product images, their titles/prices/buttons are clustered; navigation links are grouped; shipping info is near the relevant section. This helps users scan logically.

Bad Example

Diller Scofidio + Renfro

The website dsrny.com represents Diller Scofidio + Renfro (DS+R), an interdisciplinary design and architecture studio. Serving as both a portfolio and an identity platform, the site highlights the studio’s projects while offering insight into its background through “Studio” and “About” sections. It also enables visitors to filter and explore the firm’s work across a variety of typologies, themes, and scales.

  • Contrast

    Because the design is experimental, some typography, especially when overlaying busy images, risks becoming harder to read. Also, the varying light/dark tones of images can cause inconsistency in how readable a text overlay is.

  • Repetition

    Some pages or project layouts might deviate from this home page's layout weakening the uniform rhythm. If someone jumps between project pages and the home page, sometimes the variation is large, and that can feel less anchored.

  • Alignment

    Because some images or overlays may “escape” strict grid boundaries, in responsive or narrow-viewport modes some alignment may shift or feel less rigid. Also, dynamic transitions might momentarily shift alignment in ways that feel disjointed.

  • Proximity

    The images and text are all crammed together and it creates a very cramped looking page.
